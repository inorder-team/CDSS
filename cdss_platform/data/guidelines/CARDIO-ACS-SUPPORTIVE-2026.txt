APPROVED CARDIOLOGY GUIDELINE: ACS SUPPORTIVE CARE & SECONDARY PREVENTION
================================================================================

SECTION 1: OXYGEN AND AIRWAY MANAGEMENT IN ACS
Routine supplemental oxygen is not recommended for patients with ACS who have
oxygen saturation (SpO2) ≥94% breathing room air. Supplemental oxygen should
be administered if SpO2 <94%, respiratory distress is present, or high-risk
features of haemodynamic compromise exist. Hyperoxia may be harmful in
uncomplicated MI and should be avoided.

SECTION 2: PAIN AND SYMPTOM MANAGEMENT
Intravenous morphine or diamorphine may be used for refractory ischaemic chest
pain not relieved by nitrates. However, morphine may reduce the absorption and
antiplatelet effect of oral P2Y12 inhibitors (particularly ticagrelor and
clopidogrel) and should be used judiciously. Consider fentanyl as an
alternative in patients with renal impairment.

Sublingual glyceryl trinitrate (GTN) 400mcg may be used for acute pain relief.
IV nitrate infusion may be initiated for persistent pain, hypertension, or
heart failure, titrating to symptom relief while maintaining systolic BP >90mmHg.

SECTION 3: BLOOD GLUCOSE MANAGEMENT IN ACS WITH DIABETES
Hyperglycaemia on admission is common and independently associated with worse
clinical outcomes following NSTEMI, even in patients without known diabetes.
Target blood glucose range during acute ACS: 7.8 to 10 mmol/L.
Hypoglycaemia (blood glucose <4 mmol/L) must be avoided.

Insulin infusion is preferred for patients with sustained blood glucose >10
mmol/L despite oral hypoglycaemic agents. SGLT2 inhibitors should be withheld
peri-procedurally and during contrast exposure due to risk of euglycaemic
diabetic ketoacidosis. They may be restarted once the patient is clinically
stable and eating normally. Metformin should be held during acute illness
and withheld if eGFR falls below 45 mL/min/1.73m², or if contrast is used,
until renal function is confirmed stable at 48 hours post-procedure.

SECTION 4: HEART FAILURE COMPLICATING NSTEMI
Acute heart failure (Killip Class II–IV) complicating NSTEMI requires urgent
assessment. Echocardiography should be performed urgently to assess LV function
and identify mechanical complications (VSD, acute MR, free wall rupture).
Loop diuretics (furosemide IV) should be used for pulmonary oedema.
ACE inhibitor or ARB should be initiated within 24 hours if systolic BP allows.
Aldosterone antagonist (eplerenone or spironolactone) should be added if
LVEF ≤40% and no significant renal impairment or hyperkalaemia.
Potassium monitoring is critical: target K+ 4.0–5.0 mmol/L.
If K+ ≥5.5 mmol/L, withhold ACE inhibitor / ARB / aldosterone antagonist
until corrected.

SECTION 5: ARRHYTHMIA MANAGEMENT IN NSTEMI
Continuous cardiac monitoring mandatory for minimum 24 hours.
Ventricular fibrillation: immediate defibrillation per ALS protocol.
Sustained VT: DC cardioversion if haemodynamically compromised; IV amiodarone
if haemodynamically stable.
AF in ACS: rate control preferred acutely; anticoagulation required
(increased stroke risk); discuss rhythm control strategy electively.
Complete heart block or high-degree AV block: temporary pacing wire insertion.
Bradycardia with haemodynamic compromise: atropine IV; temporary pacing if
unresponsive. Avoid beta-blockers in high-degree block.

SECTION 6: RADIAL VS FEMORAL ACCESS FOR PCI IN NSTEMI
Radial access is preferred over femoral access for PCI in NSTEMI due to
significantly lower rates of access site bleeding, vascular complications, and
mortality benefit in high-risk patients. Femoral access may be required for
complex procedures (e.g. rotational atherectomy, large bore devices, IABP).

SECTION 7: TRANSITION OF CARE AND DISCHARGE PLANNING
Minimum monitoring duration: 24 hours continuous ECG post-NSTEMI.
Prior to discharge, confirm:
- Stable haemodynamics without recurrent ischaemia
- Echocardiography performed and documented
- Discharge medications reconciled (see Section 11, CARDIO-ACS-NSTEMI-2026)
- Cardiac rehabilitation referral completed
- Follow-up appointment booked (cardiology at 4–6 weeks)
- Patient education provided: symptoms of recurrent ACS, when to call 999/112
- Sick day rules for diabetes medications explained

SECTION 8: SPECIAL POPULATIONS
Elderly patients (≥75 years): Prasugrel is generally avoided due to excess
bleeding risk. Ticagrelor at standard dose or clopidogrel preferred.
Lower thresholds for early invasive strategy in high-risk elderly patients.
Women with NSTEMI: Higher rates of non-obstructive coronary disease (MINOCA);
consider vasospasm, spontaneous coronary artery dissection (SCAD). OCT/IVUS
imaging may be helpful at angiography.
Patients with prior CABG: Graft disease is common; angiography to evaluate
native vessels and grafts. Radial access from right arm if LIMA used.

SECTION 9: CONTRAINDICATIONS SUMMARY TABLE
Nitrates: Avoid if SBP <90 mmHg, RV infarction, PDE5 inhibitor use.
Aspirin: Avoid if documented allergy; consider desensitisation or alternative.
Prasugrel: Avoid if prior TIA/stroke, age ≥75, weight <60kg, eGFR <30.
Morphine: Use with caution; may reduce P2Y12 inhibitor absorption.
Beta-blockers: Avoid in acute decompensated HF, high-degree block, SBP <90.
ACE inhibitors: Avoid if hyperkalaemia >5.5, bilateral RAS, pregnancy.
Fondaparinux: Contraindicated if eGFR <20 mL/min/1.73m².
